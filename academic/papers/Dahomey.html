The Dahomey Kingdom and the Aesthetic of Complements

         According to the Merriam-Webster dictionary, the term aesthetics is used to refer to a branch of philosophy dealing with the nature of beauty, art, and taste. It is also used to refer to a particular taste for or approach to what is pleasing to the senses, especially sight. It is with these definitions that herein will begin a partial exploration of the aesthetics of the African Dahomey kingdom, and the concept will be expanded slightly, but significantly, throughout. Although every culture, society, or community has particular styles and tastes for all manner of physical things that people interact with, the foundational themes are what make them distinct and, in the case of a historical society like the Dahomey kingdom, allow us, in the present, to interpret what about these styles and tastes inspired what the people in question regarded as art or artistic. The Dahomey kingdom had aesthetics that ranged from heavily functional to heavily abstract, like other societies, but unlike some others, one of its foundational aesthetic principles was complementarity. This does not necessarily mean that there was a balance of power between the two complements, rather this was usually not the case i.e. the royal conquering lineage and the conquered, commoner lineage. Practically everything that the Dahomey created to be artistic or otherwise aesthetically pleasing, including religious or political beliefs as well as physical objects, was based on this principle of complementarity, manifesting in many ways that have a commonality or linkage inspired by the idea that there are aspects of or things in life that not only need a complementary “other” but also specifically require it in order to be valuable or functional, or in some cases, to exist at all.

         The inspiration for the complementary nature of Dahomey aesthetics can be seen as early as the most popular version of the story of the founding Dahomey. Edna Bay describes the earliest part of the myth[1] in detail:

The myth tells the story of a line descended from the mating of a princess of the royal family of Tado, in what is now Togo, with a leopard. The princess’s leopardlike son, Agasu, became the tohwiyo (mythical founder) of the clan of the Agasuvi (children of Agasu). Migrating from Tado, the Agasuvi settled in Allada, where they became the ruling lineage and hence took the name Alladahonu (people from Allada). After a succession dispute, three Agasuvi brothers separated. One remained to rule in Allada, a second went southeast to found the kingdom of Porto Novo, and the third traveled north to found Dahomey.[2]

It is the Fon royal bloodline, created from the mating of a princess and a leopard, which first introduces the idea of a complementary nature of the Fon people, the descendants of the princess and the leopard. The princess represented a royalty and the power and right to rule, whereas the leopard represented the commoner, the “people of the land” so to speak, who ought to be ruled but also revered. This is an important aspect of the Dahomey kingdom; it is essentially certain that the Fon dynasty were conquerors that came from somewhere outside of Wawe, the founding center of Dahomey on the Abomey plateau. As Bay puts it, “a relatively small band of strangers settled in Wawe and came to control it.”[3] The adjacency of conquering outsider and conquered native was not merely a coincidental piece of history for the Fon, because the mating of royalty and commoner carried forward into actual ruling practice with the introduction of the kpojito, or reign-mate, of the king. Documentary evidence and consensus support the idea that the first two kings of Dahomey, Wegbaja and Akaba did not have a kpojito, but the third king, Agaja did, as well as every king to rule Dahomey that came after.[4]

         The kpojito, often also referred to as “Queen” or “Queen Mother,” was a position that was held in Dahomey by a woman of non-royal lineage. The idea of a conquering king needing a complementary commoner reign-mate was then reflected in the architectural aesthetics of the Dahomeyan palace in Abomey, the central seat of governance. For example, the tombs of past kings in the palace were arranged clockwise from northwest to northeast, whereas the tombs of past kpojito were arranged in the opposite direction, from northwest to southeast, in a different section to the west.[5] This clearly implies that the Dahomey royalty as being more powerful than non-royals, but not necessarily superior to them. The living quarters of the king and kpojito were similarly oriented in a different part of the palace, with the quarters of the kpojito to the west of the king’s.[6] The complementarity was also displayed more prominently in a visual sense to non-Dahomeyan outsiders who visited the palace for political or economic issues such as military concerns or trade through the presence of the ahosi, the very large and very diverse crew of ascended commoners who lived at and staffed the palace. The vast majority of the ahosi were women, and some mated with the king to produce the children of the crown, the ahovi, who were therefore of royal lineage, extending further the power and influence of the Fon bloodline. When visitors saw the king, they would conduct their business in the inner courtyard, which was divided into two sections by midribs of palm branches.[7] As Bay puts it, “[the] palm branches described a line that was both sexual and political. [...] The line also underscored the palace as a separate and secret organization...of women...capable of blocking or promoting outsiders’ interests.”[8] The Dahomey kings used this arrangement to display that he had power over thousands of women who were of various non-royal lineage, which represented the entire population, and also that his power was dependent on them, since the visitor would need to, according to contemporary Europeans, “work through the women of the palace.”[9]

         The complementarity of the Dahomey does not end at the royal and non-royal paradigm. In fact, it extends into the colorful and diverse dimension of Dahomeyan religious belief as well. The Dahomey believed in an afterlife that occurs in a world of the dead called Kutome, a belief that is still held today by Dahomeyan descendants. It is similar to the Kongo Mpemba in that there exists an afterlife world that all but mirrors the land of the living, and that birth and death represent a crossing from one world to the other[10]. Kutome is populated with vodun, or spirits, many of which are linked to a human lineage,[11] and there is complementarity in both the vodun themselves, as well as the political order in Kutome. For example, vodun are neither thought of as all-powerful gods who must be feared, nor as beings whose actions are entirely divorced from human interaction and influence. The Dahomey instead thought of the spirits of the dead as needing nourishment, like us, and that sacrifice in various forms is crucial both to keep an individual vodun extant and to request its assistance.[12] Moreover, the power and influence of a healthy, venerated vodun is proportional to its lineal connection, the most powerful vodun being linked to the Fon.[13] The general life of Kutome then is very similar to life in the living world, in that the rulers need a complementary force to keep them sustained.

         Many physical objects created by the Dahomey have references to the complementarity of the authority and those under it. One very prominent art form that originated in the Dahomey kingdom and lives on today is Fon appliqued cloths, or simply Fon appliques. They are typically of a large size and, having a rectangular shape, are suitable and made for wall hangings.[14] These cloths were pictorial depictions of oral tradition, current events, or important people. Like other artistic works, Fon appliques were used as a declaration of power and identity.[15] One example of the Dahomeyan complementarity of the ruler and the ruled came in the form of appliques that depicted a dokpwega, a hereditary title for a man in charge of a work group of five men. He was a respected figure in Dahomeyan society, and these work groups were responsible for many tasks involving physical upkeep of the kingdom, especially of the palace at Abomey.[16] They could have been made to depict oppressive power, but they instead illustrate a complementary nature of manager and employees, two necessary parts of a whole. However, most royal artwork, including Fon appliques, depicted violence and death, emphasizing the power and accomplishment of the king, and were used in fantastic celebrations of the opulence of the crown.[17] One might consider this to be oppressive, but Monni Adams notes a curious contradiction:

These uses of the colorful, pictorial cloths illustrate the contradictory functions of the arts of leadership. On the one hand, this art distinguished and separated the elite from the rest of the population. On the other, it won rapport. The very means that set the elite apart was used to arouse admiration, loyalty, and pride from the people.[18]

The final product of the complementarity inherent in both Dahomeyan society and Dahomeyan aesthetics then becomes clearer: the royalty and the commoners, the rulers and the ruled, the conquerors and the conquered are all pairs of parties who recognized the need for the other and the inability to be effective without the other.
