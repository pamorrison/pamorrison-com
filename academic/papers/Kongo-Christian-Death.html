The Kongo-Christian Death : An Exploration of the Impact of Christianity on Kongo Afterlife Over Time

         How do people affect their friends and loved ones after they die?  Do they have any effect at all?  What happens in the afterlife that affects the living, and does an afterlife even exist?  What type of place do people end up in when they pass if it does?  Do good and bad people go to the same place, and if not, do they affect the living in different ways?  These are among the questions that religions tend to try to tackle, and in the case of pre- and post-colonial Africa, there is a stunning variety of answers to these questions that come from what are commonly referred to as “Traditional African Religions.” It is important to note that this term unfortunately belies the richness and sheer number of different belief systems that have existed, and still exist, in Africa, and it perhaps suggests a degree of similarity among all elements that does not exist. However, there are some specifically African religious conceptions that are very common throughout the continent.  For instance, many Traditional African Religions, including the Congolese religion (hereafter referred to as “Kongo”), have a belief in an afterlife that is specifically a world that either closely resembles or essentially mirrors the world of the living, in which ancestors and other spirits live, and this belief helps to answer the questions initially posed herein. There are other important commonalities, and because of the importance and prevalence of Christianity in African history, for better or worse, traditional African religious beliefs regarding the afterlife are juxtaposed with Christian beliefs to illustrate a contrast that raises eyebrows without raising questions, provided appropriate context, and an exploration of the evolution of the beliefs of the BaKongo people in East Africa as an exemplar can provide this context. Similar to so many historical examples of the intersection of an African religion and Christianity, Kongo has not only comingled with Christianity but also absorbed fundamental aspects of it without creating philosophical or religious dissonance.  Also, and more specifically addressing the theme “Bodies That Matter”,  in Kongo and versions of its syncretized practice, the dead continue to affect the living beyond mere memory by their actions and influence because of Mpemba, the “land of the ancestors,” the mirror world to which people transition to when they die, despite the fact that one of the core tenets of most sects of Christianity is that the final afterlife is not current and ongoing but rather will contain no human spirits or souls until some type of end-of-days scenario.  Additionally and also in line with the theme, the introduction of Christianity to the BaKongo by Portuguese missionaries long ago had a profound effect on the cultural and religious trajectory of the Kingdom of Kongo, as well as Africa more broadly, but regardless of the intentions or desires of the missionaries, Kongo was never supplanted. To the contrary, one example of the lack of impact was either blending the Heaven/Hell paradigm of Christianity and the Mpemba belief of Kongo or simply ignoring the Christian conception of the afterlife entirely.  In this way, practitioners of Kongo performed what is often referred to as “Africanizing Christianity” over the course of centuries, and the effect of this can be seen not only in distant history, starting with the evangelism of the earliest Portuguese missionaries during the initial occupation and colonization of Africa hundreds of years ago by various European societies, but also in modern times, up to and including the present day.

         The BaKongo are an ethnic group living at and near the coastal areas of parts of present day Congo, the Democratic Peoples’ Republic of Congo (formerly Zaïre), and Angola.[1]  According to many BaKongo, they originated in Mbanza Kongo (present day San Salvador in Angola), and it became the capital of what would later become the Kongo kingdom.[2] Kongo is the name of the indigenous religion[3] of the BaKongo people.  In An Anthology of Kongo Religion: Primary Texts from Lower Zaïre, John Janzen and Wyatt MacGaffey reproduced an excellent summary of the Kongo belief about the primary nature of our world, pulled from “Man in his World,” originally written by Fukiau-kia-Bunseki in 1969:

The N’Kongo thought of the earth as a mountain over a body of water which is the land of the dead, call Mpemba. In Mpemba the sun rises and sets just as it does in the land of the living. Between these two parts, the lands of the dead and the living, the water is both a passage and a great barrier.  The world, in Kongo thought, is like two mountains opposed at their bases and separated by the ocean.[4]

According to Kongo cosmology, there is a line that runs through the “great barrier” previously mentioned by Bunseki called the Kalunga line (an anglicization of KalÛnga), because it is made of the universal energy called Kalunga[5] that divides the Kongo conception of the cosmos in half, with the land of the living on one side and Mpemba on the other.  This basic image is included in the Kongo cosmogram, a religious image of the BaKongo that is a circle with two perpendicular bisecting lines, one horizontal, the Kalunga line, and one vertical, that form four points on the circle. These points visually represent four “moments” of the sun’s daily journey: dawn, noon, sunset, and also midnight, when the sun is at its noon point in Mpemba.[6]  This conception of the universe functions as symbolic for the cyclical life of a BaKongo person, or muntu[7], with each moment of the sun representing also a point in this life cycle. The rightmost point is the transition of death in Mpemba to birth in this world, the upper point is the point of most ascendant power in this world, the leftmost point is the transition of death in this world to birth in Mpemba, and the bottom point is the point of most ascendant power in Mpemba.[8]  Because this life cycle is believed to be available either to everyone or at least to “extraordinary persons” with “a life well lived,”[9] it is clear that at any point in time, the land of the dead is where people who have made the death transition live for a time, and so obviously must have its own society, culture, norms, and practices, and because this world mirrors the land of the living, its geography must match ours as well.  So then the Kongo afterlife must be alive, extant, current, and ongoing.  By contrast, the typical representation of the afterlife in most sects of Christianity is very different. Protestant Christians traditionally believe that neither Heaven nor Hell contains any human souls, and that this will not change until an “end times” transition for all of humanity occurs as interpreted from the book of Revelation in the Christian Bible.[10]  Therefore, it must be that from the Protestant perspective, when people die they must enter some kind of indefinite stasis, waiting for a day of transition to a permanent destination. The sect of Christianity most important to consider in relation to Kongo however is Catholicism. In traditional Catholic belief, there is a moment of transition (judgement) for humanity that will eventually occur, but people do not go into a static or sleep state upon death.  Rather, Catholics believe in purgatory, an area of the afterlife that either is distinct from Heaven and Hell or itself functions as Hell, in which all human beings visit and must stay until their souls become clean enough to enter Heaven.[11]  So then it is clear that the Catholic conception of the afterlife is not just different from the Kongo conception but incongruent in certain ways, especially with regard to the idea of permanence.  Kongo holds that one eventually returns from Mpemba and is reborn into our world, whereas Christians, including Catholics, believe that death brings one to a final destination, a place from which no one will ever return to the living world.  This incongruence is important to consider when investigating the history of the introduction of Catholicism to the BaKongo when they first encountered Portuguese colonists in the 15th century.

         The Kingdom of Kongo no longer exists as a political entity but starting in the 13th century and continuing until its downfall in the 17th century, it was the nation, both culturally and politically, of the BaKongo people.  The BaKongo ethnic group were initially migrants to the area they would come to occupy and at which they would create their kingdom.[12]  However, they regard their origin to be what would become the capital city of their kingdom, called Mbanza Kongo. [13]  At its zenith, the Kongo Kingdom covered a geographical area that ranged from the Atlantic coast to the Kwango river in the east and from the Congo river in the north to the Kwanza river in the south,[14] and as previously mentioned covered area in modern-day Congo, the D.R.C., and Angola.  The Portuguese first arrived in the area sometime after 1483, and the king of the Kongo Kingdom at the time was named Nzinga Zuwu, who not only immediately welcomed the foreigners but sent an emissary to Lisbon.[15] The Portuguese responded in kind by sending its own emissary to the Kongo Kingdom that included explorers, artisans, and missionaries, which prompted a response from Nzinga Zuwu:

The missionaries achieved a major victory: Nzinga Kuwu was baptized and given the name João I. However, he eventually returned to the native religion of the Kongo, one that worshipped ancestral gods. A civil war soon erupted after his death in 1506 between his sons Mpanzu a Kitia and Afonso (who took this name after being baptized a Christian). Afonso eventually took the throne in 1509, and from here he was able to begin pushing his Christian agenda.[16]

For the BaKongo then, it appears as though the voluntary state sponsored conversion to Christianity was only voluntary from the perspective of the elites, and “despite Afonso’s efforts and goals, Christianity never spread out past the elites by the time of his death in 1542,”[17] and so it can be inferred that most of the BaKongo were still practicing Kongo decades after the gesture to the Portuguese made by the ruling royalty.  However, it is not necessarily true that political leaders or even missionaries were primarily responsible for the eventual dissemination of Christianity in the Kongo Kingdom.  In fact, as John Thornton notes, “a careful reading of the original sources shows that missionaries played a relatively small role in defining Christianity, and that from very early on in its history, Kongo Christianity was shaped by educated Kongo laypeople.”[18]  This is likely because although the Portuguese missionaries carried with them the basic tenets of Catholicism, it was not possible to bring with them a substitute for a substantial portion of the culture of the BaKongo, which had an effectively complete overlap with religious thought and practice.  Indeed, in his attempt to argue a similar point about Christianity generally being subsumed by African religion, Michael Ras Brown notes that “Catholic Christianity could not equal, let alone overcome, the immense dimensions of the fully conceived and lived West-Central African religious cultures.”  In essence then, the BaKongo elites initially welcomed the arrival of Catholicism as a political and diplomatic tool, and even though they had a contentious, and then eventually violent and destructive relationship with the Portuguese, the society that brought Catholicism to their shores, many BaKongo eventually decided of their own free will that portions of the foreign Catholic belief system were worth more than mere consideration; Catholicism contained enough truth to then justify incorporating parts of it into their own religion.

         Since the metaphorical ball was passed to the laypeople of the BaKongo, it is then worth trying to delineate major areas of theological difference between the Roman Catholicism introduced by the Portuguese and Kongo, and as argued previously, the concept of the afterlife in each is one such area, and this raises a major question: what is it about the Catholic belief of the afterlife that did not prevent the BaKongo from rejecting Catholicism outright?  After all, the Portuguese missionaries, via their Catholic beliefs, were at the time attempting to teach the BaKongo that if they did not accept the Catholic interpretations of the Bible that they would go to Hell, completely divorced from both God and their ancestors forever.  One might intuit that the enthusiasm for these teachings among the BaKongo would have been very low and that royal dictum would have been the only way to force Christianity to proliferate.  However, as noted from Brown and Thornton, it was the general public of the Kongo Kingdom that voluntarily chose to accept and start practicing an Africanized version of Christianity, which contradicts this intuition. One aspect of the early Portuguese missions holds a key point of understanding, as Thornton explains:

Missionaries who went to Kongo carried with them what might be described as an inclusive conception of the religion, as opposed to an exclusive concept, such as was applied in the Spanish colonies in the Americas. The inclusive concept means that all aspects of the culture of the target country that are not directly contrary to the fundamental doctrine of the Church are considered acceptable.[19]

In addition to this, the Kongo king at the time, Diogo I, typically preferred making BaKongo people local clergy, rather than allowing the missionaries to decide, as they desired and expected, to bring in additional foreign clergy to staff churches and missions.[20]  This evidence then completes a nearly complete chain through which Christianity was initially spread into the Kongo Kingdom: the missionaries brought the message, the leaders embraced it, the average muntu liked the message and decided to learn about it, the message was inclusive, and the concept of an African, Kongo-style Christianity is born.  Unfortunately, there is no specific answer for why there was a strong desire among the BaKongo to syncretize Christianity and Kongo, but we can at least understand the basic reason why it does not make sense to argue that the BaKongo should have rejected it: there was simply no reason to.  Because of the inclusive nature of the Portuguese missionaries’ message, the BaKongo were essentially free to pick and choose what they wanted to keep and ignore the rest, and with regard to the afterlife, the Christian idea of an awful place cut off from God and their ancestors was simply rejected by most.  However, it is not the case that there were not genuine converts.  In fact, Christianity as interpreted by the BaKongo was not uniform, and it also was not binary.  In fact, a wide variety of traditions and beliefs have developed over time among the BaKongo ethnic group, primarily influenced, to varying degrees, by both Kongo and Christianity, and we can feel echoes of this in the modern day.

         Simon Mfuka wrote a doctoral dissertation about the BaManianga that provides a powerful example of this phenomenon a few decades ago.  The BaManianga are a subgroup of the BaKongo who live in “Lower-Zaire” (today’s southern Democratic Peoples’ Republic of Congo).  According to Mfuka, his informants told him that the BaManianga received their name because they “settled in a despised area, Manianga” that was known as an abominable land, although he acknowledges that John Janzen previously explained that the origin lies in the name of a long famous nearby marketplace. [21]  Because this information concerns a community that is modern, the differences between the particulars of their religious practices and historical Kongo are noticeable, but the primary elements of the afterlife and other theological areas are either the same or very similar.  For instance, the BaManianga believe in a creator God who is referred to as Nzambi [a] Mpungu, and death due to old age or sickness brought on by it as accepted as being initiated by “Nzambi Mpungu Tulendo,”[22] with Tulendo being a word that acts as a suffix of the title meaning “power,” as in the power of God.[23]  However, the BaManianga do not believe in microbial/microscopic life, thus diseases in otherwise healthy people who are not elderly are typically attributed to either an unhappy spirit being or to a person with what Mfuka calls “mysterious power.”[24]  And for any other more complex cause of death, angry or malcontented spirits are the usual suspects, with the level of suspicion being a function of the level of the disease’s complexity.[25] The BaManianga have obviously not had much influence from other cultures and societies (or perhaps have rejected it), but Mfuka does not address this, as it is not relevant to the dissertation topic.  Regardless of the reason for this, these beliefs are consistent with ancient Kongo, as are their beliefs regarding what happens to the people who do pass on.  The BaManianga believe in the land of the ancestors called Mpemba, and its aspects are essentially identical to what has already been described: Mpemba is the land of the ancestors, where one goes when one dies provided one has not behaved selfishly and has instead lived a moral life.[26]  However, one may be excluded from Mpemba upon death, and Mfuka tells the story of a man to whom this occurred.  There was (and likely still is) a Manianga clan named Kiyongi, and there was a chief of that clan at some point in the past named Mayu, whose misdeeds and selfish behavior Mfuka reproduces from witnesses and story tellers in detail.  Upon Mayu’s death, there was no eulogy or sadness but rather happiness and celebration, for he was the type of man considered to be unworthy of entering Mpemba, so egregious were his transgressions in life.[27]  Since he could not enter Mpemba, he was, after his death, seen from time to time wandering about the village, what we might call a “ghost”, which disturbed the other residents who then planted pineapple trees on his grave to drive him away, and he was never seen again;[28] this is an example of what Thompson described earlier, that Mpemba is not necessarily available for everyone, and in this case, when someone dies, he or she simply disappears eventually, being excluded from both worlds at the point of death.[29]  In addition to all the similarities to Kongo, there is no mention of any belief or ritual that appears to be drawn from Christianity, which at this point is somewhat rare for the general area, so the BaManianga exemplify the preservation of Kongo.

         On the other end of the spectrum, there is the Church of the Holy Spirit in Africa, another modern belief system held by members of the BaKongo, among many others throughout central and southern Africa, that appears both on paper and in person to be heavily influenced both by Christian Protestant evangelism and by Catholicism but which has not rejected Kongo influence entirely.  Interestingly, its central “monastic village” is in the same territory as the previous group, Manianga, meaning that among the practitioners are BaManianga.  The various Churches of the Spirit, such as the Church of the Holy Spirit in Africa, all operate similarly in that they “reject the concept of mission and the accompanying social services as well as the legal hierarchical apparatus that necessarily accompanies it.”[30]  Just like other subgroups of BaKongo, including most BaKongo identifying themselves only as part of the BaKongo and not as part of a subgroup, the branches of the Church of the Holy Spirit in Africa traces their spiritual heritage to a source, and in this case it is a man named Kimbangu.[31]  The heritage is traced through several successors, including Masamba Esaie, whose “inspiring teaching and prolific writing left an indelible imprint on the life of the church,” who also wrote many vision songs called “Songs of the Spirit”[32]  Among the topics of these songs is the subject of salvation, and the English translation of one of these songs is given in Janzen’s text: “We shall fight the battles / In the strength of the Father; / Deliver us Lord Jesus, hear us! / The thunder of the fight is loud, / Do not be afraid. / Deliver us Lord Jesus, hear us.”[33]  The act of calling to the Christian God, calling on his strength to do battle, and praying to his son Jesus are obviously ritual practices in devout Christian sects, which likely means that the practitioners of this belief system have entirely rejected (or perhaps were simply never introduced to) core tenets of Kongo.  Indeed, the members of the Church of the Holy Spirit in Africa follow the Bible closely and believe in the Heaven/Hell paradigm of the afterlife, but there are still traditions and practices inspired by Kongo, inadvertent as they might be.  There is a ritual called “Weighing of the Spirit,” performed to the heavy beating of drums that involves intense singing and rhythmic dancing; in this ritual, if a “weigher” and a “weighee” end up touching hands, the “weighee” has to confess his or her sins to a prophet, and according to the primary text, “the rite bears a close resemblance to the traditional Kongo blessing dumuna, or sala duma, also a form of jumping handshake.”[34]  And to be sure, there are hundreds of other examples of Africanized Christian churches similar to this, including many Church of the Holy Spirit branches, all over southern and central Africa, all bearing little resemblance to Kongo but still indelibly linked to it.

         The syncretism of Christianity and Kongo can also take other forms, such as religious political organizations, and one such example is Bundu Dia Kongo, or BDK, which does not seem to draw from either religion primarily but instead has a significant amount of influence from both.  The BDK was founded by a man named Ne Muanda Nsemi, who “received a call” in 1969 when he was a university student, which he initially rejected; he went on to write a sacred book called Makongo in 1989 and used this book to found BDK.[35]  The BDK advocates in political spheres for the recreation of the original Kingdom of the Kongo, in its pre-colonial geographic state, including Mbanza Kongo as its capital city, from which BaKongo originate.[36]  Regarding the religious aspects of the organization, it draws both from Protestant interpretations of the Bible as well as traditions of Kongo.  Tata Nzami’a Mpungu (God) created the universe and many groups of people on Earth, one of which is the BaKongo, and each of these groups, called makanda, have their own religions and prophets that arise in critical moments, such as Moses and Jesus, that are all legitimate and connected to Nzami’a Mpungu.[37]  The BDK believe in the traditional Christian triumvirate God, Jesus, and Holy Spirit, but the trinity symbolism does not end there.  There are also three primary aspects of Nzami’a Mpungu: love (zola), intelligence/wisdom (ngangu), and power (tulendo), and these aspects are literally spirit incarnate in the founder Ne Muanda Nsemi, as well as prophets both current and past.[38]  The BDK also draws heavily from Kongo, which is why “Kongo” is in the name.  In fact, similar to other subgroups and cults of Kongo from around the Kongo area, the BDK believe the word “Kongo” means, among others, the first name of God, the magical rainbow that accompanies self-creation of the universe, the whole black race, and the name of their kingdom.  The BDK specifically believes that Kongo is real, so although they promote the Christian origin story, their afterlife is similar to Mpemba and unlike the typical Heaven/Hell paradigm of Protestantism or Catholicism.

         The conception and meaning of death, and what happens when you die, among BaKongo has changed over time as Christianity has spread throughout the African continent. The BaKongo were the first to encounter Christianity, and although in the past scholars have promoted an idea that Christianity was thrust upon them, more recently scholars are finding that the opposite is true.  The introduction of it to the Kongo Kingdom was coercive at first, but eventually the average muntu decided to take a look and decide for herself whether or not to accept the teachings of the Portuguese missionaries and the Bible. However, regardless of how much influence Christian ideas have had on the BaKongo, in the modern-day Kongo is still very much alive, and Mpemba is full and prospering.
