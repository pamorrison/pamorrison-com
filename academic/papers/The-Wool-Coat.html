The following is a (very fake) story I wrote to demonstrate various cultural practices observed by archaeologists, such as ritual burial and reclamation. Enjoy!

 

The Wool Coat

         My father was an archaeologist.  Actually, he was a bit more of an antiquarian.  I regularly heard about the adventures he went on and the fascinating places he had been.  Typically, he brought home proof in the form of trinkets or tools, and his excursion to the Isle of Man was no exception.  The major difference this time, however, was that what he brought home was neither trinket nor tool.  It was a black coat, similar to a modern pea coat, but it was made of tough wool and had imperfect patches that I later found out covered several holes.  On top of that and oddly enough, it fit him perfectly.  Although his claim regarding how he found it likely sounds dubious, I believed it at the time, if only because of its strangeness.

         The Battle of Ronaldsway is the genesis of the story of the coat.  According to my dad, he had found out through some mysterious source that somehow a smaller part of the Manx force, despite being ultimately and wholly defeated, had had some comparative success in their ultimately defeated campaign against the Royal Scottish Forces in a location closer to the coast and not far from the primary battlefield.  Apparently fewer than a hundred men had actually fought and, to a man, died there.  My dad and his team, like so many times before, started digging without permission just outside of Castletown.  This particular spot was lightly forested a long time ago, but the area had since been cleared and replanted with grass in modern times; he found dead root fragments from the trees that had previously resided there, and these roots had disturbed some of the bones of the bodies of the men who had fallen.

         A curious and little-known custom that was part of the Manx warfare tradition was that in the rare case that a man has the option of leaving the field in a losing battle or staying there to “fulfill” one’s duty as a soldier, he should do as much to honor his people as he is physically able, including taking one’s own life.  This is where the story of “Red,” so named by my father and no one else, comes from.  Red had interpreted this custom in an interesting way: that he was dying did not stop him from honoring his people and literally burying himself.  My father found evidence in the bones, the armor, and the clothes that blood was everywhere at the time of the burial, and he theorized that Red had no way of making it back to the main battle, so Red settled on respecting the Manx tradition and the soldiers he fought against in the same gesture.  He dug a shallow grave next to a tree and donned a wool coat, likely worn by a felled richer Scotsman who had in turn bought or stolen it from an Englishman.  He wore it over some of his clothing, put on his armor over the coat, and then lie down in the grave to die.  Apparently, the victorious Scottish soldiers who were still alive, and in the area, must have allowed him to stay, and they filled in the grave, coat and all.  Over time, certain animals, insects, and microbial life had their way with the body since it was not in a casket as the body had been completely eaten away, but miraculously, this grave was relatively well preserved otherwise until my father dug it up.  He found evidence that the tree it was next to had fallen over, and some of the roots had shifted the bones and clothing, even creating holes in them.  The circumstances around the creation of the grave were at once interesting and macabre, and my father noted that Red had managed to create an individualistic ritual while also being faithful to tradition.  Near the gravesite, my father found other pieces of nearly lost history, including other skeletal remains, as well as baubles, spearheads, and other fragments of various materials.  However, he had found his macabre keepsake: the wool coat, worn by a long dead Manx warrior.

         I thought it looked ridiculous when he came home wearing it, but I smiled and accepted when he revealed his “surprise” that he was giving it to me.  Oh boy.  I cringed as I accepted the weird, gruesome gift, but it came to mean more to me than I thought it would at the time; it was a way of extending his legacy, and it was important to me because of its importance to him.  He had taken the coat to a tailor he knew personally and had it thoroughly cleaned and crudely patched.  My father was not very faithful to the science of archaeology, but he was certainly faithful to the preservation and restoration of old and ancient things.  This coat was no exception, and as strange a gift as it was, it represented the unique perspective my father had.

         It has been several years since my father passed, but when he did, I had him put on the coat one last time, and he was buried with it.  Inside one of the pockets is a plastic-wrapped note that explains the coat’s history and meaning.  I wonder if, perhaps hundreds or thousands of years from now, his grave will be unearthed by curious people, and it seems a funny thing that they will eventually discover that the coat had been buried not once, but twice.

Paul A. Morrison
