Viability of Capitalism Outside the West

         Capitalism is defined as being an economic system in which production and distribution are privately or corporately owned.  This means that, in order to qualify as capitalistic, businesses must be mostly or wholly free from government interference, coercion, and corruption.  Capitalism is also marked by free markets, and development occurs through the accumulation and reinvestment of profits gained within these markets.  The West has been financially successful with regard to the rest of the world, and capitalism could represent a successful model for the rest of the world (Esposito, West).  It instead may be difficult or impossible to implement in third world, underdeveloped, developing, and former communist nations because of the lack of legal representation of goods and wealth in objective markets (de Soto).  There is evidence to support both points of view, and the issue is complex.

         The history of the word “capitalism” is important to understand with respect to the history of the associated concept.  It was first coined in a 1788 pamphlet, Le Capitalisme Dévoilé (Capitalism Unveiled), by a French agronomist named François Ébaudy de Fresne (Merrill).  The term had little use until a major dispute between prominent socialist Louis Blanc and his former colleague Alphonse de Lamartine arose in the late 1840s, around the time of the February 1848 Revolution in France (Merrill).  After hearing criticism from de Lamartine of his political and economic beliefs and the beliefs of those with whom he was ideologically aligned, and in response to Lamartine’s idea that socialists supported suppression of capital, rather than capitalism, Blanc wrote (Merrill):

Nonsense! You confound that which socialists never confound and attribute to them your own ignorance. In every social system, capital is properly regarded as absolutely indispensable to industry and agriculture. And when it passes from serving isolated individuals to serving groups of associated individuals its utility, far from being lost, is multiplied. Rather than dissipating when so concentrated, it grows. The suppression of capitalism is not the suppression of capital.

We find then that the origin of capital is older than capitalism, and they are distinct from each other.  Capital (goods that can produce revenue) is rather a building block of many economic systems, and capitalism is one of those systems.

         Capitalism in practice necessarily is not inextricable from governmental structures like democracy, but because governments are often at ideological odds with capitalism, they clash often and forcefully (Esposito).  However, the promotion of the individual is an important tenet of both democracy, the oft-cited companion of western capitalism, and economic liberalism, the modern, global economic ideology that spawned from capitalism, which has led to many financial benefits to the developing world (West).  For example, global corporations from western capitalism can operate inside of poor nations, and the citizens, although working in what many consider very harsh environments, subjectively can improve their financial station and stave off the necessity for engaging in religious practices like arranged marriage by gaining disposable income, a powerful tool for the impoverished (West).  One of many examples includes Munni, a Bangladeshi woman whose story is featured near the end of a documentary by the BBC on the subject of arranged marriage and other extreme religious practices.  Although her life is “immeasurably hard” by our standards, the disposable income that her sweatshop labor job affords her, however small, is powerful enough to have gained her pride from her parents and a stay on an arranged marriage, which is surprising since she is merely 13 years old (West).

         In a global sense, capitalism may have keys for unlocking a future from dire poverty and hunger, although there necessarily is not consensus about the ability of capitalism to do this.  Economic liberalism, a system of thought that is a byproduct of capitalism that focuses on the individual market value of people and their capital, according to the Cato Institute’s Daniel Griswold, has been responsible for cutting global dire poverty in half in the last 150 years, when it had been nearly constant for the previous 6000 (West).  While certainly impressive, this type of economic boon may not, however, be the result of capitalism or even a capitalistic spirit, and it instead may be the result of a combination of the consolidation of capitalism and democracy (Esposito).  Moreover, this type of basic wealth increase may not represent a new path for any number of citizens, and the financial gain may be ancillary, since moneys and assets have little to no legal or market representation in the Third World (de Soto).

         Politics plays an important role in determining whether or not any economic system will succeed or fail.  Anticapitalists believe strongly in the idea that capitalism can succeed despite ethics or morality, and that it is dangerous because of this (Merrill).  Others have observed that since capitalism inside the West has seen to the growth of so much wealth, that the global morality and necessity go hand-in-hand: capitalism is necessary and right for accomplishing lifting as many out of poverty as possible (de Soto).

         Capitalism has seen very little of the world’s progress so far, since it has only existed for around 170 years.  In that time, global poverty has decreased drastically, which is the result of many factors, and although capitalism is certainly one of those factors, debate continues about the degree and significance that capitalism’s role has played in this upswing of economic representation and freedom, and time will only tell if the future holds a place for capitalism as well.
