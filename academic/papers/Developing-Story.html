Developing Story: The Adverse Effect of the 24-Hour News Cycle on Modern Journalism

         Modern journalism, reporting that involves informing the general public about events that have occurred in the very recent past via technology, has evolved greatly in its relatively short lifespan.  Walter Cronkite is an example of a modern journalist.  He was one of the earliest television reporters and America’s most famous journalist and news anchor.  He was considered to be the most trusted man in America at the height of his popularity, and he commanded CBS’ nightly half-hour news slot (Sundem).  Cronkite once said, “Our job is only to hold up the mirror - to tell and show the public what has happened” (Cronkite).  Since his reign, and largely due also to Ted Turner’s creation of the Cable News Network (CNN), journalistic standards have steadily decreased while consumption and necessity for news content have conversely increased.  The latter has led to the creation of other all-news networks, like MSNBC and the Fox News channel, whose sole purpose is to deliver news content on the back of advertisement revenue (Sundem).  Now in full swing, the destructive nature of these networks has never been more apparent or severe.  The 24-hour news cycle, a modern invention of all-news networks, does not help keep the public well-informed, rather it trades too much information for opinion, and the public should actively turn to more effective tools and news sources with higher journalistic standards in order to effect positive change in modern journalism.

         Journalists are employed all over the world by various news organizations for the purpose of generating content via reporting on what they observe.  They are expected to create reports that are written, televised, or both.  These reports are used to create content for, in large part, filling half-hour, full-hour, or even multi-hour news programs.  Journalists are invaluable for allowing the public access to unbiased, raw information.  Concordant to this access, the effect of being informed about actual and relevant events that occur in the world is what most philosophies consider to be a pillar of a progressive, fair, and learned society (Sullivan).

         When CNN, the world’s first 24-hour news station, turned on its lights in 1980, the world was not so different from how it is today.  The necessity for CNN’s 24-hour news model neither existed then nor now, since there has never been enough ongoing world news, either legitimately interesting or vital to know, that demonstrates that necessity.  However, the fact that there was any 24-hour news station available for at least short spans was a demonstrable blessing in the mid-1980s during the space shuttle flights and debacles (Folkenflik).  Indeed, since its creation, CNN and other modern equivalents have proven their worth and value as a tool for the general public for staying informed about ongoing issues and stories that concern them.  These issues might involve crises or data that necessitate critical timing in the delivery of the information.  The positive potential of 24-hour news networks is not to be underestimated.  In times of temporary but frenetic world events, e.g. war, globally reaching news networks like CNN and others can be absolutely indispensable during those times (Folkenflik).  However, they never stop broadcasting and they, like almost all other television networks, are funded through advertisements.  This creates a void that must be filled when nothing or very little is happening in the world.  What fill that void and also matches the theme or tone of these networks are mostly opinion and conjecture regarding whatever news is already being reported on.  In turn, this leads to bias being reported as fact, which is often cited as a huge blow to the integrity of journalism as a whole (Sundem). 

         The reporting associated with recent terrorist attacks viscerally demonstrates this decline in journalistic integrity.  News and journalism experts believe that tragedies should be handled with extreme prudence, because misinforming the public in the face of terror can be very dangerous.  At odds with this tenet and in the aftermath of the Boston Marathon bombing in April 2013, the all-news networks immediately began speculating about suspects (Sullivan).  The involvement of social media was both of enormous potential good (Qu) and applicably harmful, due in large part to the frenzy the attack created (Guzmán 24).  Damaging, inaccurate information was shared through Reddit and Twitter, and law enforcement agencies ended up trusting non-vetted sources of information (Guzmán 22).  The chain of journalistic evidence is often too rushed and flimsy, and sometimes only the oldest and most well respected publications, such as The New York Times, can model restraint and prudence in the face of overwhelming tragedy (Sullivan).

         Since there are yet ways that journalism can improve, average citizens have both a right and a responsibility to be involved in how journalism is handled and deciding what information is relevant and actionable.  The part of the 24-hour news cycle that is driven largely by social media has a place for the amateur journalist, and other average people have the option of simply not participating in engaging advertisements that drive profits for monolithic corporate news stations like CNN.  Becoming and staying informed regarding cogent events is entirely possible with a moderate degree of effort, so the battle for modern journalism is winnable.  However, this seemingly endless cycle is a product of all-news networks that are actively engaged in sensationalistic, opinion-based reporting.  It is therefore important to take responsibility for one’s own education.  One should focus on facts over conjecture and support the responsible use of social media tools for dissemination of information in a helpful way.  Finally, the rational news publications, those that adhere to the journalistic standards of old, must be shown support more vocally by the public.  After all, they are the ones that still hold up the mirror.
