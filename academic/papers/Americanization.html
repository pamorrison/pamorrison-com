Americanization: Early Years in America

         During the eighteenth century, many British observers remarked on what they saw as the growing cultural, economic, social, and political differences between themselves and the colonial subjects across the Atlantic.  For these British observers, the colonials were becoming more American and less British with every passing year due to a phenomenon that would eventually become known as Americanization.

         In the 17th and 18th centuries, various societal trends were responsible for forming a unique culture and a distinct people from the settlers and immigrants of the North American British colonies.  Many of these trends together form a process called Americanization.  Some of the trends that contributed to Americanization were predictable results of ethnogenesis, while others were specific to the history of the area.  This essay will explore examples that exemplify some of the major trends that occurred in the colonies, specifically clothing and architecture fashion, the formation of uniquely American economic interests, and the genesis of American revolutionary politics.

         In the early colonial period, wealthy colonists bought magazines from London and sent their children to England for schooling in an attempt to engage in Anglicization, the process of strengthening the bonds with the mother country.  However, English culture in some parts of the colonies evolved in tandem with the easily overlooked evolution of the middling colonists’ cultures; colonists produced and bought utilitarian clothing inspired by other, preexisting cultures, like the Indians.  Whereas the rich bought and wore custom wigs, ornate and ruffled clothing, and expensive, heavy coats like what was worn in England, the average colonists relied on what was available and suited the environment; they used rougher, plain cloth for shirts and coats, animal skins for hats, as well as leather and dungaree cloth for pants.  These clothing choices would become visually influential enough as to later inspire Benjamin Franklin to wear a coonskin cap and a plain, brown waistcoat in an attempt to look more American during a diplomatic mission to Versailles.  Architectural fashion ran a similar course.  Whereas the rich could afford everything from manicured English style hedges and gardens to Georgian style symmetrical buildings and houses, the average colonists would embrace the affordable and the functional; along with the claptrap, dirt floor homes of the impoverished, other foreign methods were employed, such as stilted house foundations from Africa and the ubiquitous log cabin, brought to America by German immigrants.  The various choices for clothing and architecture, made primarily by the middling colonists, inspired a unique fashion that shaped American culture and society.

         The goods that colonists bought, sold, and traded played an additional role in their own mostly unintended Americanization.  The English Parliament ironically assisted this by creating the Navigation Laws during the Commonwealth Period of 1649 to 1662 which, despite being intended to solidify an economic dependence from its colonies during the kingless turmoil, spurred economic interests and industries that ultimately assisted in destroying it.  They effected this in 2 major ways: concentrating American industrial efforts and creating an unmanageable colonial coastal trade, the former being intended and the latter not.  The Navigation Laws created subsidies for things like indigo, which was grown in the Carolinas but then exported to England for textile manufacturing.  They also created a program for the bank of England to allow merchants to extend credit to American farmers.  They even made certain things illegal to produce in the Americas, like bibles for example.  All of these had the intended effect of creating specific, booming, efficient industries, including indigo, timber, fish, wheat, and corn.  It also created the phenomenon of trade deficit, because the value of the goods being exported from the colonies was less than the value of the goods being imported from England, which the colonies needed to survive and function efficiently.  The English government addressed this by redirecting cheaper foodstuffs like wheat, corn, and dried fish to the Caribbean Islands, where British colonies of mostly enslaved Africans cheaply produced an enormous amount of coffee and sugar, which made its way back England.  This round robin style trade among these three major areas solved the problem of imbalance with regard to trade deficits and surpluses, but it also created a need for merchants in America to physically move goods to the Caribbean.  This, in turn, created an almost entirely untracked and unmanageable internal coastal trade, in which merchants would sail south along the coast, trading with every major port between its origin and destination.  According to scholarly sources, possibly as much as half of all trade that occurred in the 18th century in the American colonial area was due to this, and it helped bolster American economic independence.

         The aforementioned Commonwealth Period in England produced a small explosion of political thought and theory.  One of the most important figures in this era was John Locke, a prominent philosopher whose words and ideas profoundly influenced political thought in America.  Among Locke’s more important contributions was a then modernized version of republicanism, a theory of representative government that dates as far back as Cicero and the Roman Empire.  Locke believed that for a republic to function, public virtue was required, and the only way to ensure it was through structures and preventative measures.  He believed a republic must have a constitution with a bill of rights based on natural rights, must be limited in government power, and must have no standing army or established church.  Locke also believed in a theory of justifiable revolution, in which any government established by the people must be abolished if it becomes incapable of protecting the natural rights of life, liberty, and property.  Every single one of these ideas would later be published and partially lead to the politics that created the English “Glorious Revolution” of 1688-89.  In turn, the history and ideas about republicanism and revolution, which at the time were culturally English, were exported to America through the children of colonists.  Ironically, the English culture informed the colonial generation that would go on to bear and teach the American founding fathers, whose republicanism and revolution created the American Revolution, the penultimate and greatest expression of Americanization.
