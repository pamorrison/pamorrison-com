The First Evening I Was Introduced to My Sobriety

         I have had a difficult time with acceptance.  Very few things that happened after I graduated high school, and prior to sobriety, made sense or seemed fair.  I had a right to do what I wanted to do with my life and the seconds, minutes, hours, days, and years that occupied it, I thought.  This very simple idea began a self-destructive process, and this indignation morphed into a nightmarish beast that stomped around inside my brain like a dragon defending its lair.  I took to the bottle, and it took to me.  Then, one evening I had what felt like an epiphany, albeit a frustrating one.  I decided at once that I wanted to stop drinking but moreover, as I would later discover, that I also was tired of being sad and feeling downtrodden and put upon.  I was playing the part of a victim, and I was ready to make an honest attempt at being in control of my life.  This would prove to be difficult, the most difficult thing I have ever done, but it would be my deliverance into an existence that is now recognizably worthwhile; I became an accepting alcoholic-addict, and a sober survivor.

         I don't remember the date.  I wish I did.  Alcoholics Anonymous is full of people who "know their date."  I have one just like theirs, but the date I became sober the first time is a mystery.  However, I do remember the setting.  I was sitting in my living room, drunk again.  The air was an acrid blanket, thick and thorny with tendrils of smoke and dust and dirt and pet things.  The light simultaneously was queasily dim and stingingly bright; the burnt-out light bulbs in the ceiling and the computer monitor, with its one hundred percent brightness and contrast, were in a constant state of war.  I sat in my dull, pewter gray computer chair, clacking away on an ancient artifact with sticky squares that were liberally spotted with primary colors.  My computer mouse was similarly stained with formerly viscous byproducts from food that never reached my gluttonous mouth.  The hum from my computer, a noisy and nosy box-shaped frenemy, drowned out the top layer of audio from my television, which was being watched by my roommate, Josh.

         I was playing World of Warcraft, a popular MMORPG (Massively-Multiplayer Online Role-Playing Game), and my character was a vision.  Polished and clean, her armor, face, and figure were beautiful to look at.  The game has a character selection screen in which you can admire the results of your work: long hours slaving away at quests involving death, glory, monsters, and heroes.  The time you put into the game resulted in attaining gear and items that would make the game more engaging and exciting.  You could go out hunting for artifacts.  You could craft things for yourself or others in the game.  You could join your friends and help NPCs (non-player characters) fight injustice in dank dungeons, in gorgeous green forests, in blood-red beast-filled sandy deserts, or in stone spires atop ancient fortresses.  You could do any of those things, or you could just look.  You could look directly in front of your otherworldly Blood Elf rogue and see a smudge on the monitor.

         I saw it.  Truly, I saw the fingerprint speckled with a faint yellow.  I did not know what it was, and to this day I do not know why I was so extremely bothered by that stain in particular, but I had to clean it off, because it was in the way of my portal into this other world.  I licked my finger, stinging my tongue with some bizarre flavor, and wiped it away.  Then I looked to my left.  I don't know why, but I did, and I saw what I usually saw: a mountain of Tecate boxes full of empty cans that had been meticulously piled up behind the couch next to me like a child’s playtime fort.  But this time I experienced it differently.  I smelled the foul stench of old beer and heard the dull clunk of my hand holding an empty can hitting my desk as my arm went limp from lack of concentration.  My body leaned left, and my head rolled right, as if my bones were jelly, and I felt the realness of it all.  This was a strange feeling, and I hated it.  I hated myself for admitting that I hated it.  I hated that I could hate myself.  All I could think about was hate.  The hatred was sweet and thick and black like molasses, and it was all over me.  It was choking and comforting in the same moment.  I liked hating me and this and everything and everyone.  As my beast roared at this frustrating intrusion, I thought

         "Fuck this bullshit.  This isn't fucking fair.  Why can't I just keep drinking?  Stupid fucking brain."

         And then I noticed I was crying.  Deep sorrow welled up inside me like a balloon.  I could feel the air around me as if it was a wall I was being pressed against.  My sadness turned into panic, and I managed to whimper

         "Josh?"

         My roommate was a character.  He was fiery and passionate in one moment and silly in the next.  His bushy, long dirty-blond hair and thick beard were completely unfit to obscure an animated face that communicated a wealth of emotion.  He was a Redwood amongst firs in most company, and he even made me feel uneasily short at times, towering over most people at 6’5”.  Josh and I had many conversations over the years that involved a range of topics.  We talked at length about politics, faith, god, love, sex, money, work, play, and vice; sometimes we invoked all of these in one session.  He was a man that I trusted and trust still.  He was a true friend, and when he saw my face after hearing me call his name, his face twisted in agonized concern.

         "I think I need to stop drinking.  If I asked you to throw away anything to drink I brought home, would you do that?" I asked with shame in my eyes.

         "Yes.  Absolutely." he said without hesitation.

         I didn't do much the rest of that evening.  The decision that I had made came from somewhere I didn’t understand, and I was all but crippled by the confusion.  I think I continued to play the game for a little while, but I realized it had lost its magic.  I eventually went to bed and woke up afterward feeling terrible.  I felt so alone, and now I had asked this guy with whom I lived to watch and control my behavior.  This was going to suck.

         Over the following few years and up until today, life has been a series of lessons.  After so many years of different variations on a thematically unchanging routine, nearly everything I did was like I had never done it before.  Shopping for and eating food from a grocery store was new.  Getting proper identification, like my social security card and driver license, was new.  Paying for a car, getting promoted, falling in love with a girl, being broken up with by a girl, and even returning to drinking all were as they had felt when I was young.  Acceptance of myself as an alcoholic and acceptance of the necessity for all these new procedures and experiences in a general sense was new as well, and I adjusted, although I cannot explain how.

         As strange as it may have seemed to me in the past, I am now on a road to recovery that involves being responsible for and accountable to my own happiness and the happiness and safety of my friends, coworkers, and family.  A major ingredient of the gravelly, uncomfortable pavement lining that road is the act of accepting who I am, who I am not, who I can be, and whom I can never or no longer be.  Acceptance of these things has been difficult for me, but I now can breathe easier when I calmly explain to new people whom and what an alcoholic is and why I am one.  Sobriety has allowed me to survive my addiction and my indignant beast and accepting my own worth has inspired me to move past them into what seems to be a very exciting future.  I have learned the power of control through acceptance of what I can and cannot change.  Hi.  My name is Paul, and I’m an alcoholic.

Paul A. Morrison
