Abstract: This piece is a response to the article A Black feminist-inspired archaeology? and specifically addresses the 3 following questions:

1. According to Franklin, what are some of the principles of Black Feminist theory and how does she enact these principles in her article?

2. How does Franklin's discussion of gender compare and contrast with the discussion of gender in your textbook?

3. What reasons does Franklin provide for why all archaeologists should be engaged with Black Feminist theory for interpreting archaeological remains?

 

In Response To A Black feminist-inspired archaeology? by Maria Franklin

         According to Franklin, Black feminist theory was borne of the Black feminist movement, which sought to combat the exclusion of Black women from second-wave feminism and liberate Black women from various forms of oppression. It has a body of core themes found in a particular subset of feminist literature, and Black feminists maintain a bounded space for their voices, because feminism typically promotes a focus only on combatting sexism, which disproportionately serves middle-class white women. One of the essential assertions of Black feminism is that Black women exist in the intersection of racism, sexism, classism, and, for Black lesbians, homophobia. Viewing these forms of oppression as an intersection, rather than as individual axes, provides a better contextual understanding of the perspectives of African-American women both in the present and in the past. In fact, Black feminist theory further asserts that this intersection is not merely an additive effect but rather an overarching structure that is distinct from other oppressive structures and produces its own set of difficulties. Another aspect is the collection of negative stereotypes associated with Black womanhood, which Black feminist theory was created, in part, to identify and combat, such as the “mammy,” and the “welfare queen.” Black feminism tells us that Black women have been marginalized and devalued in the past in ways that are specific to them, so promoting and empowering Black women to tell their stories is a way to more accurately understand their historical perspectives and experiences.

         Franklin’s audience is clearly academic and as such does not define gender anywhere, but the way she discusses gender is consistent with the textbook. Gender is a socially constructed vision of aspects, strengths and weaknesses, or “inherent” qualities directly tied to biological sex. The textbook gives many examples of non-contemporary gender roles, inherited from gender ideology, that were prescribed for and/or performed by men and women, such as the Plains Indians berdaches. Franklin draws from this conceptual definition of gender to discuss the gender role and gendered activities prescribed for Black women that created, in part, their location at the intersection of the aforementioned forms of oppression. One way that Franklin differs from the textbook regarding gender is that she does not distinguish between gender in a broad sense and its applications, gender role and gender ideology. Rather, Franklin assumes that this is known to the reader and moves through discussions of ideology and role fluidly. However, she never contradicts the textbook’s definitions or descriptions.

         Black feminist theory has a particular advantage in contextualizing both previously racialized and previously gendered interpretations of the past. Similar to archaeology’s recent increase in effectiveness due to the inclusion of oral histories of indigenous peoples, Franklin argues that Black feminist theory provides similar tools that would allow more archaeologists to appropriately interpret and correctly assess the history of not just Black women but also other marginalized groups. Because Black women, especially in the plantation South, experienced a great variety of hardships in the past that are to some degree still occurring today, archaeology can be more effective by using social theory that seeks understanding and explaining marginalized peoples’ experiences. Because human beings’ interactions are so strongly dependent on preconceived notions of race and gender, it is important for archaeology, as a bastion of historical knowledge, to vigorously investigate as many aspects of individual lives in the past as it can, and Franklin argues that Black feminist theory is an unfortunately neglected toolset that can positively redefine how archaeologists ask questions and form theories about what happened in the past, what it meant then, and what it means today.
