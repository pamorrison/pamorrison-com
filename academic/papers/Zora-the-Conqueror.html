Zora The Conqueror

         She left for up north, and then she came back.  She traveled all around so she could get a piece of herself recorded on paper.  She took her home with her wherever she went, and she never forgot where she came from or who she was.  Zora Neale Hurston, the charming, brilliant, and enigmatic author, was at once both a novelist and an anthropologist by profession. She dealt in fiction and nonfiction with a proficiency that was uncommonly impressive in her own time and is significantly inspirational now.  Her works have influenced many authors, such as Alice Walker, Toni Morrison, and Maya Angelou.  In fact, we owe Hurston’s rediscovery and current recognition to Alice Walker, who made it a personal mission to have Hurston’s work recognized after she had found that Hurston was literally “the only trained black anthropologist of African American life and culture in 1930s America, as well as an award-winning writer known for the distinctiveness of her voice” (Miller).  This distinctiveness allowed Hurston to publish a culturally invaluable literary anthropology, Mules and Men (1935), and a highly acclaimed novel, Their Eyes Were Watching God (1937), within two years of each other.  Although she was certainly an accomplished novelist, it is her anthropology and ethnography that truly drove her spirit and inspired her to be a cataloguist for black culture.  Throughout her career, Zora Neale Hurston sought to create and recreate narratives about African Americans, attempting to build her own space in the literary world by sharing her culture and values as a black woman from the south.   She combated racist stereotypes of black folks by showing that they have a large range of emotions, religious expressions, ideologies and political and financial achievements, unlike what was assumed by many white folks at the time, and she exposed the world to many distinctive qualities of African American culture and of “Negro religion,” as she called it, and she illustrated just how closely linked these were.

         Hurston was born in northern Florida in the small town of Eatonville in 1891.  At the time, Eatonville was an interesting exception to the average American city in that it was all black.  Eatonville’s mayor, marshal, ministers, shop owners, denizens, etc. were all African American.  According to Hurston, “the only white folks were those who passed through” (Hemenway 12).  Because of this, Hurston’s childhood was also uncommon for a black girl growing up in the south.  She was insulated from the horrors of racism in the early part of her childhood in Eatonville, before her various hardships began to occur, and the loving, supportive environment in which she grew up as a child helped her to define herself as something other than a victim of tragedy.  She would go on to write “Drenched in Light,” a short story about a little girl fictionally adjacent to Hurston named Isie Watts.  In this story, Hurston fictionalizes her own early childhood through Isie’s experiences, and it is clear that, although not without some heartbreak, Hurston’s childhood was mostly happy and relatively normal.  Isie, like Hurston, was loving, laughing, joyful, and more than a little precocious.  When it was published, “Drenched in Light” was criticized for its simplicity, but Hurston did not intend to tell a complex story.  Rather, she declared to the world through this and many of her other works that she was an exception that disproved the rule.  Black folks were not without tragedy, but their lives and identities were neither defined nor consumed by it.  It was Hurston’s strong belief that all it took for a black child to grow up healthy was a community free from oppression, which she never considered an unreasonable expectation.

         Although Hurston did not experience racial oppression in her early childhood, she differed from Isie Watts in that she experienced a trauma that would significantly alter her path in life.  When Hurston was around nine years old, her mother, Lucy Hurston, passed away.  Her mother had sometimes been an imperfect caregiver, but Zora loved her and was devoted to her completely.  When Lucy was near death and bedridden, she called Zora in to give her very specific instructions.  Lucy told her that “no one was to take a pillow from under her head as she was dying, and neither the clock nor the looking glass in the room was to be covered with cloth” (Hemenway 16).  Zora did not understand this strange request, but she internalized it without question.  She would later realize that these seemingly odd acts were components of a ceremony that her community engaged in to prevent a “hard” death, so that the spirit would not haunt the living.  Surprisingly, her mother was “symbolically reject[ing] the folklore of her village” (Hemenway 16).  However, Zora’s father physically restrained her despite her protests about the pillow, the clock, and the looking glass during the death ceremony, and what Lucy had requested that Zora prevent happened anyway.  Hurston carried the resentment and trauma that came from being unable to fulfill her mother’s last request for many years after.  She said that it was “the end of a phase in [her] life” and that “That hour began my wanderings.  Not so much in geography, but in time.  Then not so much in time as in spirit” (Hemenway 17).  This was the moment that Hurston’s connection to her own community was partially fractured, and in her “wanderings” she sought to find a way for her small-town spirituality to coexist with her big-city aspirations.  She was forced to overcome significant hardships following her mother’s death, but she went on to succeed in this mission.

         After she became an adult, and while working a number of odd jobs and graduating from two different educational institutions, including being awarded an Associate’s degree at Howard University, Hurston began to truly write in earnest. Her published works such as “Drenched in Light” and “Spunk” led to a secretary/chauffeur/companion position with a successful novelist, Fannie Hurst, which then led to Hurston meeting Annie Meyer, one of the founders of Barnard College, Columbia University’s women’s division.  Meyer created a scholarship for Hurston so she could attend Barnard free of charge (Hemenway 21).  Next to her mother’s passing, this was arguably the most important moment in Hurston’s life; “The Barnard experience was critical to Hurston’s development, for she came to New York in 1925 as a writer and left Barnard two years later as a serious social scientist...” (Hemenway 21).  As a student at Barnard College in New York, Hurston also contributed significantly to the Harlem Renaissance, both with her presence and with her work.  Her reputation as an extremely charming and witty storyteller is well documented, and she began blending her writing with the folklore that she had carried with her from Florida.  Her first “attempt to fuse folklore with fiction” (Hemenway 68), “The Eatonville Anthology” was published in 1926, and it was filled with the stories from her past she had used to entertain almost everyone she met; it was a “literary equivalent of Hurston’s memorable performances at parties” (Hemenway 69).  Although she was experiencing an early personal renaissance as an artist, she eventually came under the tutelage of Franz Boas of Columbia’s Anthropology Department. This altered Hurston’s academic direction, and she was inspired to train as an anthropologist, because “his science provided a taxonomy for her childhood memories” (Hemenway 63).  Less than two years later, in late February 1927, Hurston had secured a $1,400 research scholarship and was boarding a train.  Her plan was to spend six months in the field doing anthropological research in central Florida, recording “the songs, customs, tales, superstitions, lies, jokes, dances, and games of Afro-American folklore” (Hemenway 84).  From this autoethnographic research would come her seminal anthropological work.

         Hurston lent her unique and empathetic voice to the otherwise dispassionate science of early anthropology in Mules and Men, a literarily significant collection of African American folklore.  Hurston first visited her hometown of Eatonville to begin the book, and over the course of her travel around Eatonville, and later Polk County nearby, she collected folk tales and religious stories.  Hurston was careful to contextualize these stories by writing the book autobiographically, finely detailing the conversations that surrounded them.  In fact, she was afraid that her work would appear too saccharine and perhaps unscientific, which was expressed in a letter to her mentor, Franz Boas.  Boas allayed this fear by responding, “It is the great merit of Miss Hurston's work that she entered into the homely life of the southern Negro as one of them and was fully accepted as such by the companions of her childhood” (Archive “Plot Summaries”).  Hurston chronicled many traditions of black folks in Mules and Men and her insertion into them.  One amusing situation has Hurston involved in a “toe party,” which was a community gathering in which women would stand behind a curtain and whose time for the evening would be bid on for a dime.  The male bidders would glimpse only the toe of the woman they were bidding on.  In Mules and Men, Hurston describes throwing her own “toe” in the ring:

Well, my toe went on the line with the rest and it was sold five times during the party.  Every[ ]time a toe was sold there was a great flurry before the curtain.  Each man eager to see what he had got, and whether the other men would envy him or ridicule him.  One or two fellows ungallantly ran out of the door rather than treat the girls whose toe they had bought... (31)

Gatherings like these occur regularly in Mules and Men, replete with tall tales, dancing and music, and they provide some understanding for how important communal connection and tradition was for the folks Hurston encounters.  The most famous of these community-driven traditions is called “lying.” Although it may appear contradictory to call it as such, lying is an oral tradition that involves telling one’s truth in order to impart lessons about hubris, generosity, and other strengths and foibles in human nature.  “Lies” are both honest and wise tales that “we tell when we’re jus’ sittin’ around here on the store porch...,” according to Hurston’s friend B. Moseley in Eatonville (Hurston 23).  They are also a part of the larger religious expression of black folks, and Hurston collected some seventy folk tales that created a rich picture of what her people in Florida thought about life, love, God, and morality, all of which are intertwined with African American spirituality and culture.  This richness was certainly exemplified in part by the personalities and lifestyles of the people of Eatonville and Polk County.  Hurston met or reunited with all manner of folks during her research for Mules and Men, and she allowed the people to speak their own truth.  There was no single version of a black person but rather a complex tapestry of folks woven from a diverse community.  Hurston wrote about meeting roughly thirty different people in Eatonville alone, all of whom she named and described in detail, who had greatly varying levels of monetary wealth, family size, and happiness, and who all had plenty of “lies” to tell.

         It is certainly obvious that spirituality and religious expression are important to African Americans, but among Hurston’s objectives was not only to explain that this is so but also to explore why they are so deeply embedded in the culture.  A posthumously printed collection of her work called The Sanctified Church contained a number of essays that she had written prior to the publishing of Mules and Men (they were published individually in Negro: An Anthology in 1934), and in them she describes in meticulous detail a great many examples of religious expression that African Americans engaged in.  In doing so, she also directly connects this religious expression to music, a cultural touchstone.  In “Spirituals and Neo-Spirituals,” Hurston calls spirituals “unceasing variations around a theme” (Hurston 79).  She went on to say that spirituals “cover a wide range of subjects from a peeve at gossipers to Death and Judgment” (Hurston 79).  It was important to Hurston that her readership understand that “neo-spirituals” are the versions of these songs that are sung outside of church, and that authentic spirituals have their own unique style.  As Hurston puts it, “The jagged harmony is what makes it, and it ceases to be what it was when this is absent” (Hurston 80).  It was for this reason therefore that the music and rhythm, along with the presence of the community and the church, was absolutely necessary for spirituals to exist, as well as many other key aspects of culture for black folks.  Hurston described another way that music was intertwined with religious expression in “Shouting.”  She explained: “Broadly speaking, shouting is an emotional explosion, responsive to rhythm.  It is called forth by...sung rhythm...spoken rhythm...humming rhythm...foot-patting or hand-clapping...” (Hurston 91).  However, it is not only about music, because “Shouting is a community thing.  It thrives in concert” (Hurston 91).  Here again, Hurston is expressing the connection between music, an appreciation for which is culturally intrinsic, and religious expression, the need for which is innate in African Americans. 

         She continued her exploration and creation of African American narratives with an almost supernatural fervor.  Hurston was an extremely prolific author who created a veritable mountain of fiction and non-fiction literature, including anthropological studies, novels, short stories, and political essays.  It is unfortunate then that her work was not celebrated in her own time, and that she often struggled to make ends meet.  Through much of her later professional life, she encountered mild to drastic conflict because of the societal gap between what she came from and what she wanted to be known for.  Although risky for her professional reputation at times, Hurston overcame some of her difficulties by presenting herself in different ways to suit various situations.  On many occasions, for example, she fabricated a birth year: “Depending on the document, and whether she was trying to impress someone with her youth or her age, she claimed to be born in 1898, 1899, 1900, 1901, 1902, or 1903” (Hemenway 13).  It is well known now that her actual birth year was 1891.  Later in life, during a divorce, Hemenway notes that Hurston’s account of her own conduct doesn’t seem truthful, given her personality: “Certainly both sides posed for legal portraits; it takes a considerable act of imagination to see Zora as she represented herself in the complaint, a ‘meek and humble type easy to be imposed upon’...” (Hemenway 273).  Hurston also modified her dialect at times, and there are stories of her doing this outside of her autobiographical writing, but it is also present throughout Mules and Men.  This is called code switching nowadays, but Hurston used it well before this official name came about, and she managed to use it effectively and sensitively to move fluidly from group to group both in her writing and in her life.

         This practice of temporary disguise was neither malicious nor even unfair; her intent was never to gain something she didn’t deserve.  Rather, it was keeping in a tradition of revealing truth through deception.  Hurston defined her career by creating and manipulating narratives about African Americans for the purpose of revealing the truth and soul of their character, so it comes as no surprise that she would do the same for herself.  Karen McCarthy Brown explored this practice in her essay, “Making Wanga: Reality Constructions and the Magical Manipulation of Power.”  The term “wanga” refers to a religious expression and ritual in Vodou, usually centered on a physical charm, in which a desired outcome is “worked” for; “Vodou priests and priestesses make wanga to help clients with loves, health, money, and not infrequently, legal problems...” (Brown 180).  Brown describes the case of Abner Louima, a Haitian man who was violently assaulted by a police officer in the 1990s.  Louima had a spiritual healer named Mama Lola “working the wanga” for him as the “word wanga of politicians, journalists, and lawyers” worked against him (Brown 191).  Brown’s argument can be distilled into the idea that facts without context can cloud truth, and that sometimes concealment is the only way to reveal it.  It is then easy to draw a connecting line from Brown’s “word wanga” to Hurston’s alternative narratives of self, then to her narratives of African Americans, and finally to the truthful “lying” of Eatonville.  All of these are directly related to the need of the community, the one that Hurston is attempting to empower and promote, for concealment and for the protection of its identity by disallowing outsiders from seeing some aspect that they wouldn’t understand and that would therefore betray the truth of the community’s character.  Hurston prophetically recognized this early in her career, and it inspired a lifelong journey toward encouraging the world to see African Americans as she saw them: regular folks with dreams who populated a joyful, powerful, interconnected community.

           Possessing an uncommon intellect, Zora Neale Hurston was a powerful author with more to say than one lifetime allowed.  Hurston wrote over fifty individual pieces of literature while she was alive, and collections of her many unpublished or out of print works have been shared since her passing (Archive).  While it may be easy to think of her as a tragic figure, having died seemingly unfulfilled, financially broke, and alone, it is clear from Hurston’s stories that she would desire no one’s pity.  In fact, some celebrate her memory in a very different way.  In 1943, Hurston wrote about a mythic hero in African American folklore in “High John de Conquer,” the titular character being also commonly referred to as “High John the Conqueror.”  He is also the namesake of a popular root used for good luck and good health.  Hurston describes when High John decides to retire:

So after a while, freedom came.  Therefore High John de Conquer has not walked the winds of America for seventy-five years now.  His people had their freedom, their laugh and their song.  They have traded it to other Americans for things they could use, like education and property, and acceptance.  High John knew that that was the way it would be, so he could retire with his secret smile into the soil of the South and wait. (Hurston 78)

Hurston inadvertently describes herself in some ways through this story.  In a practical sense, she came from nowhere on a mission to help her people, the black folks of America.  She came to see racial oppression and injustice, but she did not run from it.  She saw America through a hopeful lens and set out to tell her stories and let the truth reveal itself to the people.  Alice Walker recognized this and started researching Hurston’s life and works after reading a copy of Their Eyes Were Watching God that was lent to her by her neighbor.  She sought Hurston’s spirit by making a sojourn to her resting place:

In an act of veneration and appreciation, Walker paid the ultimate respect to Hurston on that trip-she located her unmarked grave in the snake-infested high grass of the Garden of Heavenly Rest in Fort Pierce, Florida, and placed a stone marker on it:

Zora Neale Hurston

‘A Genius of the South’

Novelist  Folklorist  Anthropologist

1901-1960 (Miller).

Zora Neale Hurston has not walked the winds of America for a long time now, but her people have their freedom, their laugh, and their song.  Walker is certainly owed credit for restoring Hurston’s legacy, and providing America, for all time, with the root of Zora the Conqueror.

 

Bibliography

Brown, Karen McCarthy. “Making Wanga: Reality Constructions and the Magical Manipulation of Power.” Religion and Healing in America. ed. Linda L. Barnes and Susan Sered. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2005, 173-194.

                  In this essay, Karen McCarthy Brown takes on the cases of Abner Louima and Amadou Diallo, two men of color who both had upsetting, unjust, and extremely violent encounters with New York City police officers.  Louima was severely injured during an arrest, and Diallo was shot dead outside his apartment.  Brown deconstructs the media coverage surrounding these cases and makes an argument that is summarized in this piece.  The primary idea she creates, regarding concealment and truth, is an aspect of African American culture that was used in this piece to contextualize Hurston’s work and professional interest.

Hemenway, Robert E. Zora Neale Hurston : A Literary Biography. Urbana: Chicago, 1977. Print.       

                  The life and works of Zora Neale Hurston are explored in this account of her life and of her art.  The author does not claim that it is a definitive work, yet it provides many important details of her life.  Hurston was a complex and enigmatic person who was fiercely committed to her work, and this book functions as an honest testament to her personality, her history, and the most important of her accomplishments.  This book was the primary source for information about the details of Hurston’s life, as well as providing some context to understand her personality and motives.

Hurston, Zora N. Mules and Men. New York : Perennial Library, New York, 1990. Print.

                  This is one of Zora Neale Hurston’s perennial works.  It is one of the earliest collections of African American folklore and is regarded as a premier entry in the genre.  In this book, Hurston transcribes a personal journey, a mission, in which she travels to various places to collect folk tales and religious and spiritual stories that her people have handed down.  It reads almost like a novel, and it has a light-hearted and lyrical style, although it is never fantastical or unbelievable.  This was one of two major works that is extensively discussed in this piece.

Hurston, Zora N. “High John De Conquer.” The Sanctified Church. Berkley: Turtle Island, 1981, 91-94. Print.

                  “High John De Conquer,” also known as “High John the Conqueror,” is a folk hero in the African American community.  He is a spirit, a trickster, and a force for good, and he is described by Hurston in this interpretation of his legend as having accompanied slaves from Africa by riding on the wind that powered the sails of the ships they were transported in.  It is important to understand how a folk hero is celebrated, and Hurston’s legacy, as resurrected by Alice Walker, is similar.

Hurston, Zora N. “Shouting.” The Sanctified Church. Berkley : Turtle Island, 1981, 91-94. Print.

                  The second essay under discussion is Hurston’s piece about “shouting,” which is another religious expression that typically occurs in African American churches.  Hurston explains the various ways that this ritual takes place and transcribes many different versions of a shout as she had observed it happening.  This essay was valuable because of Hurston’s providing an additional connection from religion to rhythm.  Hurston carefully explains, likely because it was not understood at the time, that shouting is not merely a backwards possession ritual but rather a fusion of culture and religious expression.

Hurston, Zora N. “Spirituals and Neo-Spirituals.” The Sanctified Church. Berkley: Turtle Island, 1981, 79-84. Print.

                  This essay by Zora Neale Hurston explores many aspects of the spiritual, a church song style popularized by African Americans.  Hurston details every nuance of the spiritual, from its jagged harmony to is individual nature and lack of reproducibility.  She also discusses the “neo-spiritual,” a facsimile that people at that time mistakenly believed was authentic, the myth of which Hurston dispels.  She provides critical information that is discussed in this piece regarding the connection between music and culture for African Americans.

Miller, Monica. “Archaeology of a Classic.” Barnard College. Barnard Coll., 17 Dec. 2012. Web. 15 Dec. 2017. https://barnard.edu/news/archaeology-classic-celebrating-zora-neale-hurston-28#Miller2

                  Monica Miller is an Associate Professor of English at Barnard College, the alma mater of Zora Neale Hurston.  This article contains a short essay that Miller wrote as a contribution to Barnard Magazine for the celebration of the 75th anniversary of the publication of Their Eyes Were Watching God.  It provided a small but significant amount of insight into the life of Hurston.  The most significant piece of information gleaned from this article was the importance of Alice Walker’s contribution to Hurston’s legacy.  Miller describes Walker’s search for the grave of Zora Neale Hurston and how finding it has changed the course of literature for African American women.

Zora Neale Hurston Digital Archive. The Center for Humanities and Digital Research, Univ. of Central FL, 2017. Web. 12 Dec. 2017. <http://chdr.cah.ucf.edu/hurstonarchive/>

This website is an academic collection of Zora Neale Hurston’s works and some histories centered around them.  It is sponsored by a major public university and provides an objective view of Hurston.  It includes sections about Hurston’s personal and professional history, copies of her manuscripts, summaries of many of her larger works, and also a section that offers criticism of her contributions.  This resource was used both to gain a better understanding of a few of her individual works as well as making the titles of everything she wrote available to easily find.
