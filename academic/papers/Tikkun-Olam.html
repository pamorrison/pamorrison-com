“Tikkun Olam”: Repairing the World

         The word “tikkun” in Hebrew means “repairing” or “rectification”.  This word is often used in the texts of Judaism to represent the act of fixing problems of law and procedure to help society avoid disharmony.  Within much of the language that involves rectifying law and procedure is the phrase “mip'nei tikkun ha’olam”, which translates to “for the sake of tikkun (the repairing/rectification) of the world.”  The word “olam” translates to or sometimes “universe”, as ancient people did not understand as much as we now know about the breadth of the physical space we occupy.  Additionally, as our understanding about our world has evolved, so too has the Jewish idea of repairing it and the reason why it should be done.  The modern interpretation of tikkun olam is similar to the modern humanistic ideal of societal contribution for the sake of increasing the wellbeing of all people.

         The earliest written citations of mip'nei tikkun ha’olam occur in the Mishnah, which is a book-form collection of Jewish oral traditions.  These oral traditions represented the basic tenets of non-biblical early Judaism.  Judaism was not merely a spiritual religious practice as it has become for most practitioners today.  In the early Rabbinic period, Judaism represented a holistic view of the practice and application of every aspect of social order, based on the instructions from God in the Bible.  It was, in other words, a way of life.  Judaism governed ritual, religion, business practice, ethical behavior, and dietary restrictions.  The Mishnah was written to preserve the oral traditions, in case the elders and rabbis, the ones whose job it was to keep the information collected in memory, ever became persecuted and killed.  Then, what was initially a collection of idioms about living a good and proper life became an authoritative text that was outside of biblical law; it was the first of its kind.  Examples of mip'nei tikkun ha’olam include spreading information about divorce agreements outside of the town in which the divorce was performed to prevent accidental unlawful remarriage, creating laws to prevent confusion about selling religious articles to non-Jews to prevent them from being resold outside of Jewish territory, and limiting payment to redeem captives for the prevention of extended and torturous isolation.  The idea behind the type of practices that qualified as tikkun olam thusly was created by the Mishnah, and because the Mishnah was not considered biblical law, the idea became tradition and was subject to an evolving interpretation, the modern form of which includes far more than rectifying local ordinance.

         Reform Judaism is one of the current and widely accepted and practiced versions of the religion, and tikkun olam has become a foundational aspect of it.  Repairing the world is not a concept that Reform Jews believe is something that should be confined to sorting out matters of civil and common law.  They believe that repairing the world is something to be done for the sake of all, so that God’s creation can be honored.  Tikkun olam helps the individual, the recipients, and the world in general, if the act so qualifies.  Modern examples of this are charitable donations, neighborly sacrifice, electoral participation, and advocacy for peace and tolerance.  These acts help the individual via a sense of satisfaction and spiritual intimacy with God.  They help the recipients or adjacent members of society via inspiration and modeling of good works.  And finally, they help the world in general via what modern Jews believe is a restorative, healing process that celebrates God in a way that cannot happen through mere words or worship.

         However, for many, honoring the Jewish God is not reason enough to perform acts that repair the world.  This is why tikkun olam, as a principle, has been developed over time as defining acts that are rewarding unto themselves with only basic human empathy as a necessity.  Christians, Muslims, Buddhists, and even Atheists, can act in accordance with the tradition, and often do unintentionally.  Even when charitable or community minded acts are not done with the intention of repairing the world for the sake of becoming closer to God, they reward the individual and society anyway.  In this way, tikkun olam is a tool that a person or group of any religious or non-religious backgrounds can use to participate in, because it improves, by its nature, the lives of the participants and the lives of the people around them.

         Repairing or restoring is a process through which something changes from a state of disharmony to a state of harmony.  The harmony created by applying the aforementioned healing process helps things or people affected by it.  In the case of tikkun olam, there is an underlying and inherent assumption; the world needs to be cared for, looked after, and preserved by the people who occupy it so that harmony will be visited on everyone.  Reform Jews believe that this celebrates and honors God because of how it helped the human roots of their culture and religion live more peaceful and harmonious lives.  They believe that a relic has well informed the reform movement by keeping a tradition that works in creating balance in society.  Both Jews and non-Jews can take comfort in the idea that, although tikkun olam is the past informing the present, repairing the world is the present helping the future and is needed from all peoples, regardless of ideology, and likely for many generations to come.
