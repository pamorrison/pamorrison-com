Does Forensic Anthropology Partially Prove that Race Exists?

         The people who work in the field of forensic anthropology are responsible for observing and recording details of skeletal remains, typically for police investigations, and using this data to categorize or classify the person to whom the remains once belonged. Among the many ways to classify her or him is “race.” This is a common trait that must be included in a report that is expected to assist in, for example, missing person investigations. However, other subfields of anthropology, and the anthropologists who practice in them, do not operate on the assumption of the existence of a classification of human beings inside the taxon of species. In short, “race” in humans does not exist, according to most modern anthropologists.

         Such was not always the case. Almost all anthropologists until the last half-century viewed the idea of human beings having well defined, distinct, deterministic groupings as basic and sound biological theory. The “Big Three” of these groupings that were used heavily in the past are White/Caucasian, Black/Negroid, and Asiatic/Mongoloid (which includes Native American Indians). In the 1960s, C. Loring Brace and Frank Livingstone challenged the existence of these groupings, arguing that races in humans did not actually exist. They posited that the variability of physical traits of human beings made attempting to create classifications based on trait groups unscientific. Moreover, from the perspective of a biologist, there simply was not enough consistency to create a coherent classification system more specific than human being. The challenges from Brace and Livingstone as well as others in the years after created a sea change in anthropology such that, by the late 1970s, the majority of anthropologists, both cultural and physical, believed in the non-existence of biological races in human beings, and this trend has continued.

         This raises questions about the use of race in forensic anthropology today. If races do not exist, then why does forensic anthropology still use them to classify remains? Furthermore, why does using this classification accomplish the job of matching remains to identities work so well, given the assumed uselessness of a race classification? The answers to these questions do not lie in an unsolvable conundrum but rather how we view the determinations of forensic anthropologists. Race is one trait among many that forensic anthropologists must decide, based on a large, recognized collection of skeletons, and these traits are not determined by science, necessarily. They are instead better understood as cultural markers that allow for the identification of people while they are alive, and these markers persist after death. So then the act of matching skeletal remains to these cultural markers is best described as a translation from biology to culture and not as a prescriptive scientific act. Forensic anthropology does not make specific claims about the existence of race outside of its subfield, and so this translation is merely a validation of the techniques inside of its subfield identifying well-known and easily recognizable variations in the morphological characteristics of human beings, which is already common knowledge in not just anthropology but the general public.

         There is no denying that these variations e.g. skin color, size/shape of facial features, etc. exist and are recognizable to people. The mere act of recognizing them and using them to classify ourselves in our day-to-day lives is part of our processing reality via culture. This involves creating names like “White” and “Black,” but it is important for anthropologists to view this through the lens of culture and recognize that using these terms, as well as race, are not scientific simply because we continue to use them. Until such time that race is replaced with something less problematic, such as “ancestry,” it is important that anthropologists continue to clarify that the use of race in forensic anthropology is not a validation of the old claim that human beings are divisible by biological race.
